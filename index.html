Parametric feature decoding

Using EEG scalp recordings.

Human scalp electroencephalography (EEG) is sensitive to electrical activity inside the brain, and it thought to reflect the synchronous activity of vast populations of pyramidal neurons, providing a masco-scopic view on brain activity. Recently, studies have shown that the information across an array of electrodes can be used in multivariate analyses to decode spatially overlapping neural information such as stimulus orientation, spatial location, spatial frequency, and motion direction (Stokes et al. 2015 TiCS ; King & Dehaene, 2014 TiCS; Sandhaeger et al. 2019 elife; Wolff et al. 2015 front syst neurosci; Foster et al. 2017 curr biol).

  • Oxford University, UK
  • PhD project

Perceptual Manifolds

Review .

After we are presented with the visual image of an object, a cascade of neural computations allows the observer to recognise and identify the object. Still much remains unknown about the coding mechanisms through which the visual system solves object recognition. Recent neurocomputational theories describe how intrinsic manifolds, defined by population activity at different stages of the visual hierarchy, allow for fine grained perceptual discriminations. The current aim is to suggest how 1) intrinsic manifolds develop through perceptual learning, 2) transformations between manifold respresentations between visual areas can inform us about perception and 3) recurrent connections between cortical areas influence the navigation through the neural space.

  • Date: January 2017
  • Princeton University, USA
  • MSc project

Multivariate Pattern Analysis: Expectations and Working Memory

functional MRI.

Previous studies have indicated that the early visual cortex plays a functional role in maintaining sensory information in working memory. We hypothesised that delay-related activity patterns represent top-down perceptual predictions rather than a sensory representation of working memory. We applied MVPA to decode, from multiband fMRI data (TR = 680 ms), the perceptual representation of working memory and perceptual expectations in the early visual cortex. In experiment 1, participants (N = 15) performed a working-memory task with a predictable distractor at the end of the maintenance period. We showed that sensory representations in the early visual cortex during the working memory maintenance period represent working memory and not expectation. Interestingly, we found a suppression of the expected distractor early in the trial. In experiment 2, only on half of the trials participants (N = 16) had to remember one of the presented stimuli. The representation of the expected distractor remained suppressed throughout the maintenance period on trials without WM. Suppression of the distractor representation was also observed prior to trial onset. We conclude that the delay-related activity patterns in the early visual cortex code for working memory and not expectation. Potentially, suppression of the expected distractor acts as an adaptive process to prevent interference of the distractor with the working memory representation.

  • Date: Jan 2016 - Nov 2016
  • Donders Institute for Cognitive Neuroimaging, NL
  • MSc project
